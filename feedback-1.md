## **Accuracy check of the main factual claims**

### **1\) Death of John Bachar**

**Claim:** He died on **July 5, 2009**, after a fall while soloing at Dike Wall near Mammoth Lakes; he was **52**.  
 **Verdict: Accurate.** Multiple reports state he died July 5, 2009, at Dike Wall, age 52\.

**Claim:** “The route was rated **5.9**.”  
 **Verdict: Not well-supported / likely overstated.** Public reporting I can access reliably confirms the location (Dike Wall) but usually **does not specify the exact route/grade of the fatal climb**. The area contains moderate routes (including sub‑5.10 options), but pinning the fatal route at “5.9” needs a primary source (accident report, witness statement, or a contemporary report naming the line).

**Claim:** It was “well below his maximum ability.”  
 **Verdict: Reasonable but partly interpretive.** There is reporting that he climbed extremely hard (one report relays that he climbed up to 5.13 over his career, attributed to another outlet), which makes a moderate-grade solo “below maximum” plausible, but “maximum ability” is still somewhat subjective (peak, at the time, solo vs roped, etc.).

## **Evidence quality issues (where the article overreaches)**

### **A) Physiology section: plausible direction, weak quantitative grounding**

Your core idea—**strength may feel preserved while fine motor control/force steadiness degrades with age**—is plausible and aligns with broader motor-control aging literature (older adults show reduced force control/steadiness and coordination).

But several of your numerical claims are not properly supported:

* “Age does not significantly influence maximal force in trained climbers’ finger flexors” (your Fryer et al. citation) **might** be true, but the linked abstract page is not readily accessible, and your text then attributes additional specific findings (e.g., RFD impairment) that I could not verify from accessible material.

* “Elite practitioners retain **80–90%** of peak grip strength into their 50s” (via a general website) is presented as hard data; it needs a peer-reviewed or clearly quantified source.

* “Proprioceptive error increases by **1.5–2 degrees per decade** after 40” and “**12–18%** increase in foot placement variability” are very specific; your \[S024\] is not actually provided as a link here, so this is effectively **uncited**.

**Bottom line:** the mechanism you propose is plausible; the numeric precision, as written, is not defensible from the citations provided.

### **B) “Statistical fog” section: correctly identifies the denominator problem, then contradicts it**

You correctly describe the **denominator problem** (you don’t know the number of successful solos/attempts, so risk rates are hard to compute). After that, you present precise numbers:

* “8a.nu represents \<0.2% of total climbers”

* “\<1,000 elite (5.12+) unroped climbers worldwide”

* “AAC data 1951–2022: **153** recorded free solo deaths”

* “fatality rate \~1 in 5,000–10,000 ascents”

* “100–200× more deadly per ascent than roped climbing”

**Verdict: These are not corroborated from the sources you cite and (given the stated denominator problem) are methodologically inconsistent.** If you truly cannot estimate the denominator, you cannot credibly state a per-ascent fatality rate with that level of specificity without a transparent model and assumptions.
